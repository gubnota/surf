Stolen Picasso posted to New York by FedEx

The 1911 painting La Coiffeuse, worth at least $2.5m, was found by US customs officers in a parcel marked Joyeux Noël

A stolen Picasso painting that had been missing for more than 10 years has been found in the US after it was posted to New York in a parcel marked “Happy Christmas”.

The package, labelled as “art craft/toy” with a value described as $37 (£24), arrived in Newark from Belgium in a FedEx shipment just before Christmas, marked Joyeux Noël.

But when American customs officers examined it, they found a Picasso cubist masterwork worth at least $2.5m. The 1911 painting, La Coiffeuse (The Hairdresser), had been registered missing from a storeroom at the Centre Georges Pompidou in Paris in 2001.

The US attorney for the Eastern District of New York, Loretta Lynch, has filed a civil suit to return the painting to France.

On 17 December, someone named “Robert” with an address in Belgium shipped the oil painting, which measured 33cm by 46cm, to a climate-controlled warehouse in Long Island City. The next day, the painting arrived at the Port of Newark and was seized. The identity of the person to whom it was sent has not been released.

The painting, which belongs to the French national collection, was last shown in Munich in 1998. It was then placed in storage at the Pompidou centre in Paris but staff realised it was missing when a loan request was made by another museum in 2001.

Banksy’s tour of a ruined Gaza

The street artist, who previously created works on the wall of the West Bank, has made a series of new paintings across ruins in Palestine

The Bristolian street artist Banksy has returned to Palestine to create another series of works, following his famous 2005 series painted on the West Bank barrier wall.

The works, which he trailed on his Instagram account last night, include one piece which somewhat resembles Rodin’s sculpture The Thinker, with the figure’s hand gone from thoughtfully supporting his face to covering it in despair – or perhaps Niobe, the classical figure weeping for the loss of her children. Another features a kitten sprayed on the remains of a wall, posed playing with a coiled ball of rusted metal as if it were wool. A third features children swinging around a watchtower as though it were a fairground ride.

The artist made a film to go alongside the works, documenting the devastation wrought by Israeli militia and bombing campaigns. “Make this the year YOU discover a new destination,” he sarcastically writes in its captions, recalling the banal exhortations of holiday brochures. One local is recorded saying of the kitten painting: “This cat tells the whole world that she is missing joy in her life. The cat found something to play with. What about our children?” The camera then rests on text that reads: “If we wash our hands of the conflict between the powerful and the powerless we side with the powerful – we don’t remain neutral.”

The artist’s previous work in Gaza on the barrier wall that seals off the Palestinian territories from Israel included an image depicting a group of girls being lifted aloft my balloons. The now infamous image marked the moment where the artist widened his satire from social problems in the UK to international issues.

Picasso’s granddaughter selling off paintings of his worth £200m

There are multiple reasons for selling masterpieces – making money undoubtedly being one of them. In the case of Marina Picasso, granddaughter of Pablo, the motive appears to be a type of revenge.

Revenge on her grandfather, who mostly ignored her during his lifetime and let her grow up in near-poverty.

In a book in 2001, Picasso: My Grandfather, Marina said that the artist had no interest in children and “drove everyone who got near him to despair”.

Ms Picasso, 64, has let it be known this week that she is willing to accept offers privately for up to seven of her grandfather’s works worth an estimated £200m. She also wants to sell the artist’s famous villa near Cannes, La Californie.

More tips from art portraits about drawing faces

Before drawing art portraits you should first consider some basics. Particularly important when drawing a face are the proportions. Of course everyone is different, but many basic proportions are always approximately equal and there are typical mistakes that a beginner often commits.

For example, the height of the forehead and the position of the eyes. Often the forehead is drawn intuitively to a minimum, and the eyes up too far on the face. The eyes are located approximately in the middle of the face. Maybe it’s the hair that deceives one in their own perception. When I started to draw faces and people, this error often undermined my art portraits.

As a kind of unit when drawing faces the nose can be used. The eyes divide the face in half. The forehead is about two noses high, the mouth + chin are also a nose high.

Portrait Painting Techniques

The creation of a portrait is a delightful, though not an easy task, because in order to achieve a satisfactory result, along with the correct technique, a good eye and a keen sense for proportionality is necessary. A successful portrait is the individual expression of the portrayed person in an apt way again, so that the particular person is recognised immediately.

If you want to paint portraits, you need to examine the various painting and drawing techniques. The beginner would do well for the time being to practice with a soft pencil before he ventures on to a colored paintings. One of the main difficulties in portrait painting lies in the installation of the correct proportions. Which can quickly lead to a catastrophic result, a slightly crooked eye or a midline lying mouth. The pair of eyes should therefore always be on one line and the center of the lips with the tip of the nose are on a length.
